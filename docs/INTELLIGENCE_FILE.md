# Restaurant Social Graphic Design Intelligence File
### A canonical reference for Blue Mezcal Management Group — Azteca / Blue Mezcal / Jackson House

## How to use this file

This document is a **three-in-one reference**: (1) a long-form intelligence briefing a human can read once and internalize, (2) a structured "Claude Designs Intelligence File" another Claude instance can ingest to look up decisions, and (3) a Nano Banana / Gemini image-prompt framework with worked examples. Everything is calibrated to three brands:

- **Azteca** — family Mexican, two locations. Register: *playful-maximalist to warm-vernacular.* Anchors: La Bamba Ice Cream, Taqueria Ramirez, La Contenta, Cielito Querido Café.
- **Blue Mezcal** — upscale agave-forward. Register: *editorial-modernist to moody-nocturnal.* Anchors: Casa Azul NYC, Handshake Speakeasy, Pujol, Licorería Limantour, Cosme.
- **Jackson House** — American tavern. Register: *warm-vernacular, heritage-editorial.* Anchors: Gramercy Tavern, Frenchette, The NoMad, Gage & Tollner.

Rule of thumb: before designing anything, name the **register** out loud. Register first, format second, copy third. A tacos-tuesday post executed in Jackson House's register is wrong even if every color is right, and vice versa. Every section below ends in a **Decision cue** block that tells another Claude when to apply what.

# PART I — LONG-FORM INTELLIGENCE REPORT

## 1. Composition & layout rules

### Focal point theory for food + type overlays
A great food post has **one focal point, one supporting beat, and one resting zone.** If your eye doesn't know where to land within 300ms, the post is dead. The focal point is almost always the hottest spot on the plate — the crust blister, the yolk break, the sauce pool — not the center of the plate. Place that focal point on a **rule-of-thirds intersection** (upper-left or lower-right intersection for Western left-to-right reading) or along a **1:1.618 golden section** from the right edge. Centering the focal point reads flat and amateur unless you're doing deliberate symmetric ceremony (a single pinwheel lasagna, a perfect martini) — and even then, off-center by 3–5% to keep it alive.

Type overlays fight food for attention. The two-zone solution: carve the canvas into a **food zone** and a **type zone** that do not overlap, ideally separated by a **quiet diagonal or a 60/40 split**. Top-tier example: Frenchette's menu-card posts cede the top third to serif headline + hairline rule, bottom two-thirds to the dish. Amateur example: centering a headline *over* the plate's focal point, forcing a drop shadow to save legibility.

### Grids, white space, asymmetry
The **12-column grid** is a magazine inheritance — Order Design, Commission, Made Thought all design Instagram posts on it. For a 1080×1080 square, that's 90px columns with 24–32px gutters; for 1080×1350 portrait, 90px columns with 28–36px gutters. A **6-column grid** works for simpler posts (menu tiles, quote posts). A **modular grid** (4 columns × 5 rows) is the editorial-magazine move for carousels — every slide sits on the same skeleton so the whole carousel reads as one document.

**White space is a luxury signal.** Fine-dining and editorial restaurants routinely leave **60–85% of canvas empty**. Eleven Madison Park posts single dishes at ~15% of canvas size. Lilia sits a hero pasta on a marble slab with breathing room on all four sides. Maximalist brands (MFG, La Bamba) do the opposite — **95% canvas density** with layered ornament. Both work. **Mid-range density (50–70% fill) is the danger zone** — it reads like a casual Canva template.

**Asymmetry wins 70% of the time** in food. Symmetric composition is for ceremony: the whole duck, the full bar shelf, the centered wordmark. Asymmetric composition (S-curve of a pasta ribbon, diagonal cut of a sandwich, off-center plate with a utensil entering from the edge) produces motion. **S-curves and diagonals** both create reading motion and depth; the cut of a knife across the frame is a leading line you can exploit.

### Reading paths on mobile-first feeds
On a square 1080×1080, the eye runs a **Z-pattern**: top-left → top-right → diagonal to bottom-left → bottom-right. Put the strongest hook top-left, a supporting element top-right, a texture/color beat bottom-left, the CTA or logo bottom-right. On a portrait 4:5 (the highest-reach IG format in 2026), use an **F-pattern**: strong headline top, supporting content middle-left, image right or full-width, CTA or logo at the bottom margin.

### Safe zones (pixel-accurate)
- **IG square**: 1080×1080. Keep critical content inside the central **940×940**. Feed crop may show only **1080×566** on the profile grid — plan the center third accordingly.
- **IG portrait**: 1080×1350. Safe zone is the central **1020×1290**. On the grid, Instagram displays the **center square** of a portrait, so critical content for grid preview must sit in the central 1080×1080 region.
- **Stories / Reels / TikTok vertical**: 1080×1920. Top UI bar eats the top **~250px**; bottom UI/captions/stickers eat the bottom **~340px**. **Keep text and logos inside 1080 × 1340, roughly 220–1560 vertical.**
- **Reels cover (grid display)**: Design for **1080×1920 full bleed** but ensure the "cover crop" — the central 1080×1350 — reads as the grid thumbnail.

### Text over busy food photography
Four reliable solutions, in order of taste:
1. **Compose for negative space.** Stage the photo with a pre-built quiet zone (a corner of linen, an empty plate edge, wall behind) so text sits on clean surface, no scrim required.
2. **Color-block field.** Drop a cream, cobalt, or charcoal rectangle over part of the image and set type inside it. Casa Azul NYC's editorial cobalt works this way.
3. **Soft radial blur/scrim.** A 10–20% opacity radial darken behind type. Use sparingly and directional, never flat.
4. **Knockout / reverse print.** Type cut out of a color field, food shows through the counters — editorial-magazine classic.

**Never** use a drop-shadowed text slapped on top of busy food. That's the single most common amateur tell.

### Foreground–midground–background + depth
Photograph food with three z-planes: a prop entering the foreground (linen, hand, glass rim), the subject in the midground sharp, the background soft with bokeh or motion blur. A **50mm or 85mm equivalent at f/2.8–f/4** gives the dimensional separation that separates restaurant-grade photography from flat iPhone shots.

**Decision cue:** Azteca playful-maximalist posts can push to **80–95% canvas fill** with layered ornament. Blue Mezcal editorial posts should sit at **40–65% fill** with deliberate breathing room. Jackson House tavern posts live at **55–75% fill** — confident but not empty.

## 2. Typography

### Pairing principles (opinionated)
A good pairing has **three contrasts**: classification (serif vs. sans), weight (bold vs. regular or regular vs. thin), and mood (warm vs. neutral, or elegant vs. utilitarian). Two serifs can pair (a wide display serif with a narrow text serif) but it takes taste — easier to get wrong than right. **Never pair two sans-serifs of similar proportion** — it reads as either indecision or accidental. Scripts and handwritten faces are **accent only**, never body or repeating headline — treat them like a signature stamp.

### Register-specific typeface recommendations

| Register | Display serif | Text sans | Accent / script |
|---|---|---|---|
| Editorial modernist (Blue Mezcal hero) | **Canela, GT Sectra, Domaine Display, Tiempos Headline, Recoleta** | **Söhne, Neue Haas Grotesk, GT America, ABC Diatype** | **Caslon Italic, Sainte Colombe** — for quotes only |
| Warm tavern (Jackson House) | **Farnham, Sentinel, Caslon, Cheltenham, Caponi** | **Knockout, Trade Gothic, Marr Sans, GT America Cond** | handwritten chalk script for specials — sparingly |
| Playful maximalist (Azteca) | **Obviously, Migra Italic, Druk, Monument Extended, Sharp Grotesk Ultra, Cooper Black** | **General Sans, Aktiv Grotesk, Gilroy** | **Dahlia, Melodrama, custom brush lettering** |
| Moody nocturnal (Blue Mezcal after-dark) | **Bodoni 72, Didot, GT Sectra Fine, Ogg** | **ABC Diatype, Söhne Breit** | foil-style faux-metallic lettering |
| Coastal/fresh (summer menu) | **Recoleta Light, Canela Deck** | **Söhne Leicht, General Sans Light** | handwritten script accents |

For Nano Banana prompts, **describe the type stylistically** (weight + classification + finish), not by name — the model does not reliably recognize specific font names.

### Size hierarchy rules
For a 1080×1080 post:
- **Display headline**: 120–200px cap height. Display serif weight black/bold.
- **Sub-headline / dish name**: 48–72px cap height.
- **Body / description**: 28–36px, leading 1.35–1.45.
- **Caption / credit / legal**: 18–24px, leading 1.3, tracked +20 to +60 em units in all-caps for legibility.

Contrast ratio between display and body should be **at least 3:1**, ideally 4:1 or 5:1. A display at 150px over body at 30px is a 5:1 ratio — confident.

### Tracking and leading
- **Display serifs**: tracking **−10 to −30 em** (tighter than default). Display type was drawn for display; default spacing is padded.
- **All-caps labels**: tracking **+40 to +120 em**. Never set all-caps at 0 — it reads like a shout.
- **Body text**: tracking **0 to +10 em**. Leading **1.3–1.5** — 1.4 is safest default.
- **Display headline leading**: **1.05–1.15**. Tight leading makes big type feel intentional; loose leading makes big type feel floaty.

### Optical size, mixed case, italics
If the typeface offers **optical sizes** (Canela, Tiempos, GT Sectra), use the correct one: Display for 60px+, Text for body, Caption for small. Ignoring optical size is the mark of an amateur — display cut at caption size has too-thin strokes, text cut at display size looks stubby.

**Mixed case reads premium; all-caps reads shouty.** All-caps is for labels, not sentences. A 60-character sentence set in all-caps is a visual scream. Exception: editorial small-caps at tight tracking — reads classical.

**Italics work when drawn.** A true italic (a different cut, not a slanted upright) in Canela, Caslon, or GT Sectra is one of the most elegant tools in the kit. An oblique upright is flat. **Script type should be used at large sizes only (80px+)** and never in all-caps. Avoid wedding-invitation script for anything but one-word flourish (a chef signature, "salud," "open").

### Type-as-image and kinetic type
The 2026 move: **display type at 50–90% of canvas**, treated as the primary visual element with photography reduced to an accent or texture. Works when the name/phrase is short and strong — "SALUD," "TACO TUESDAY," "OPEN." For Reels covers, hold this still for 0.5s then animate: kerning expansion, color pulse on a single letter, or a hard cut to the dish.

**Decision cue:** Azteca can flex between chunky display (Cooper/Obviously/Sharp Grotesk) and hand-lettered vernacular; Blue Mezcal anchors on a single editorial serif (Canela or GT Sectra) + one quiet sans; Jackson House anchors on an old-style or slab serif (Farnham/Sentinel/Caslon) + Knockout or Trade Gothic all-caps labels.

## 3. Color systems

### How color behaves on food
Warm reds, oranges, ambers, and golds **enhance appetite and read as comfort.** Cool blues, greens, and whites read as **premium, fresh, coastal, clean** — ideal for seafood, cocktails, produce-forward dishes. Black reads **nocturnal and luxury**. Cream and bone read **editorial and natural**. Neon-saturated anything reads **playful/casual**.

Restaurants that break the "warm = appetizing" rule do it deliberately: Casa Azul's cobalt signals *modern editorial Mexican*, not fresh seafood. Use a cool palette on a warm dish only if the editorial tension is intentional.

### Palette construction (with archetypes)

**60/30/10 rule for restaurants:** 60% dominant (the canvas / background / room), 30% secondary (typography, surfaces, secondary graphic blocks), 10% accent (the signature pop). Amateur posts distribute color evenly — 33/33/33 — and read confused.

**Archetype palettes** (hex):

1. **Editorial modernist (Blue Mezcal primary)**
   - Cobalt `#1E3A8A` | Bone `#F3ECD8` | Marigold `#F2A900` | Agave green `#4E6B3A` | Charred black `#1C1A17`
   - 60% bone, 30% cobalt, 10% marigold. Black/green as occasional pulls.

2. **Moody nocturnal (Blue Mezcal after-dark)**
   - Charred black `#0B0B0B` | Brass `#C9A24B` | Velvet burgundy `#4A1621` | Smoke gray `#2A2825` | Warm white `#EDE4D3`
   - 60% black, 30% brass/burgundy mix, 10% warm white.

3. **Warm tavern (Jackson House)**
   - Forest `#1F3B2D` | Oxblood `#5B1A1A` | Cream `#F2EADF` | Walnut `#3B2A1E` | Mustard `#C8962F`
   - 60% cream, 30% forest OR oxblood (pick one dominant), 10% mustard.

4. **Playful maximalist (Azteca)**
   - Hot pink `#FF5BA7` | Sun yellow `#FFD400` | Turquoise `#1FB5B0` | Chile red `#E63946` | Cream `#F8F2DF`
   - Inverts 60/30/10: pick two hero colors at 40/40, supporting color at 15, accent at 5.

5. **Earthy/natural (coastal fresh, produce-forward)**
   - Sage `#A8B48A` | Terracotta `#C96F4A` | Bone `#F0ECE2` | Ocean blue `#4A6E7A` | Black olive `#2E3326`

6. **Mexican/Latin heritage (Azteca secondary + Blue Mezcal folk)**
   - Cobalt `#1A3FA8` | Marigold `#F2A900` | Chile red `#D72638` | Masa cream `#F3ECD8` | Oxblood `#6E1423` | Turquoise `#1FB5B0`

### Pulling a palette from a hero photo
Process: sample **five colors** from the hero image — the brightest highlight, the darkest shadow, the dominant midtone, the signature color (the hero ingredient), and one subtle accent (a prop, a surface). Push saturation on the signature color **+15%** and pull saturation on the neutrals **−20%** to create hierarchy. This yields a palette that feels *of* the photograph, not applied to it.

### Saturation discipline
**100% saturation everywhere = amateur.** A professional palette has a single saturated hero color paired with muted neutrals. Look at La Bamba carefully: the pink and yellow are saturated, but the photography is shot against cream, the type is often cream, and the composition balances the saturation with calm zones. Even maximalism has rhythm.

**Muted vs. punchy rule:** If the photography is moody/low-light (Frenchette, Handshake), mute the graphics (oxblood, brass, cream). If the photography is bright/daylight (Dimes, Via Carota), the graphics can carry more color. **Never do loud graphics over loud photography** — pick one to shout.

### CMYK vs. RGB
Social is RGB — design at sRGB. Hex codes above are RGB. Matchbook merch, menus, printed collateral go CMYK — do a **soft-proof conversion** before handoff; saturated RGB oranges and cobalts muddy in CMYK (`#FF5BA7` hot pink in CMYK is ~15% duller). For print-faithful social (the "matchbook aesthetic" MFG loves), mute your digital palette 10–15% to match the print mood.

**Decision cue:** If another Claude sees "upscale mezcal hero," use **Editorial modernist + Moody nocturnal blend** (cobalt + bone + marigold, or black + brass + warm white). If "taco tuesday Azteca," use **Playful maximalist**. If "Jackson House brunch," use **Warm tavern** with mustard dominance.

## 4. Textures, gradients, surface treatments

### When gradients work
- **Subtle tonal gradient on a solid color** (cobalt `#1E3A8A` → `#1A3275`, ~5% shift) — adds life to a flat field without looking Canva.
- **Film-grain gradient** — a slight vignette plus grain overlay makes digital feel analog.
- **Paper-texture gradient** — cream paper lightens toward the center, darkens at the edges — reads letterpress/risograph.

### When gradients fail
Any rainbow gradient. Any cyan-to-magenta gradient. Any gradient that spans more than 40° of the color wheel. Any gradient mesh with soft-pastel "2014 tech startup" energy. These are instant amateur tells.

### Textures that buy you premium
- **Noise/grain overlay at 3–8% opacity** — makes flat color feel photographic. Non-negotiable for anything aiming editorial.
- **Paper textures**: cream laid paper, kraft, linen, stucco, hand-made deckle-edge. Sit at 8–15% overlay or as the base surface.
- **Risograph**: off-register 2-color print texture, fluorescent pink + blue, slight misprint. Zine-authentic, huge in 2024–26 natural-wine/bakery/coffee world.
- **Halftone dots** — 45° angle, variable dot size. A nostalgic newspaper feel. Works for Azteca vernacular register, never for Blue Mezcal editorial.
- **Film grain** (35mm Portra emulation) — shifts photography from "phone" to "editorial."
- **Stucco / adobe / tile** — physical texture photographed as background, dropped behind type.

### Drop shadows: amateur vs. pro
Amateur: default Photoshop drop shadow, 5px offset, 5px blur, black 75% opacity, 90° angle. Generic, flat, digital. Pro parameters for directional premium shadow:
- **Offset**: 0–2px x, 4–8px y (always more Y than X)
- **Blur**: 16–40px
- **Opacity**: 8–20%
- **Color**: NOT black — use a darker version of the background color (e.g., cream bg `#F2EADF` → shadow `#6B5A3A` at 12%)
- **Spread**: 0

For type over photography, use a **long soft shadow** (y-offset 12–20px, blur 30–60px, opacity 10–15%) or an inner glow instead. **No 1990s bevel-and-emboss**, ever.

### Duotone and tritone
Duotone (two colors mapping shadows + highlights) is a trusted editorial move. Cobalt shadows + cream highlights = Casa Azul territory. Oxblood shadows + cream highlights = Frenchette. Use for feature photography, chef portraits, behind-the-scenes — not for hero food (you lose the ingredient colors). Tritone adds a midtone color — more depth, more craft.

### Embossed and inner-shadow looks
Inner shadow on type over a paper texture = **letterpress emboss**. Parameters: inner shadow 2px y offset, 3px blur, 50% opacity, color `#6B5A3A`. Very warm tavern. Use sparingly.

**Decision cue:** Blue Mezcal gets **noise overlay + subtle paper texture + tonal gradient**. Azteca gets **halftone + risograph + stickered-over maximalism**. Jackson House gets **cream paper + letterpress emboss + film grain on photography**.

## 5. Hierarchy & information design

### The three information zones
Every post has **primary (the hook)**, **secondary (the context)**, and **tertiary (the proof / logo / legal)**. The 3-second scan test: in 3 seconds, can a viewer get the primary? If no, the post is failing its first job.

- **Primary** = the single biggest element. Hero photo OR display headline. Takes 40–60% of visual weight.
- **Secondary** = supporting copy. Dish name, date, price. 20–30% weight.
- **Tertiary** = logo, small legal, URL. 5–15% weight.

### Specific layouts by post intent

**Reservation promo**:
- Primary: display headline ("TUESDAY. 7PM. ONE TABLE LEFT.") OR moody interior photograph
- Secondary: small-caps label ("RESERVATIONS") + dish/occasion info
- Tertiary: booking URL or "link in bio," logo bottom
- Placement: headline upper third or dead center; CTA bottom margin, never center

**Ingredient breakdown**:
- Primary: overhead hero of the plated dish
- Secondary: hand-drawn arrows or thin rule-line callouts pointing to each component with tiny labels
- Tertiary: dish name bottom-left, logo bottom-right
- La Bamba's signature move is this format in playful register; Bon Appétit does it in editorial

**Quote post**:
- Primary: the quote itself, set as display type, taking 60–75% of the canvas
- Secondary: attribution, smaller, italic or small-caps, 2–4 lines below
- Tertiary: logo or mark in corner
- Never add a decorative photo behind a quote post — the type IS the image

**Announcement (hours, events)**:
- Primary: the number, time, or date treated as display type
- Secondary: context ("HAPPY HOUR. M–F. 4–7.")
- Tertiary: logo
- Menu-card / ticket / matchbook aesthetic works here

### CTA placement and styling
CTAs should read **premium, not ad-copy.** Never a full-width button with rounded corners — that's e-commerce. Instead:
- **Small-caps italic** ("reservations at link") at bottom margin, left or center
- **Hairline-underlined text** that reads like a magazine dek
- **Hand-drawn arrow + handwritten note** (Azteca register)
- **Telegraph-style boxed text** (Jackson House register)

### Logo placement strategy
- **Corner lockup** (bottom-right is default; bottom-center for symmetry) at 40–80px wide on 1080 square — never larger.
- **Integrated watermark** — logo burned into a paper-texture edge or subtle pattern.
- **Full-brand moment** — the whole post IS the logo (launch posts, anniversary).
- **Omit entirely** — for editorial-restraint brands (Estela, Lilia register). If the post is a dish or a quote, the logo may add nothing. Trust the feed context to carry the brand.

**Decision cue:** If the post is one of twenty in a month, place logo subtly or omit. If it's a campaign launch, the logo gets a full moment. Never put logo dead-center over food.

### Formatting dates, times, prices
- Time: **5 – 7 PM** or **5–7pm** (en-dash, no space around for tight, space-en-space for editorial). Never "5:00pm - 7:00pm."
- Date: **Tuesday 14.5** or **Tue, May 14** or **5.14** — pick one style and keep it consistent across all posts for a month. Editorial register favors the numerical format with periods.
- Price: **$14** preferred over **$14.00**. Tasting menu: **$125 / 5 courses**.

## 6. Post format taxonomy

For each format below: **pro version | amateur version | composition cue | type cue | exemplar.**

**Hero dish spotlight** — Pro: single plate, asymmetric crop, natural light with one clean shadow, prop restraint, name in sub-headline beneath. Amateur: centered symmetric plate on patterned tablecloth, over-saturated, "YUMMY!!" caption. Composition: rule-of-thirds focal, 4:5 portrait. Type: small serif dish name, italic accent for region/pairing. Exemplar: **Lilia, Via Carota, Don Angie lasagna**.

**Ingredient breakdown / anatomy of a dish** — Pro: overhead flat-lay with components fanned; thin lines or numbers labeling each; one-color palette with single accent. Amateur: five food icons floating, Comic Sans arrows. Composition: overhead 1:1, components arranged in a visual sentence. Type: tiny labels, Gilroy Light or a handwritten script. Exemplar: **La Bamba (playful) / Bon Appétit (editorial)**.

**Chef's / staff pick** — Pro: three-quarter portrait of the person holding or adjacent to the dish, warm tungsten light, small serif quote beneath. Amateur: plate alone + stock "CHEF'S PICK" badge. Composition: 4:5 portrait, subject eyes at upper third. Exemplar: **Gramercy Tavern's chef series, The Breslin**.

**Quote post (review / chef / literary)** — Pro: oversized serif display type on a single color field, tight leading, small attribution. Amateur: quote over random food photo with drop shadow. Composition: type at 60–80% of canvas. Type: Canela / GT Sectra / Bodoni at 140–200px. Exemplar: **Atelier Crenn's menu-as-poetry format**.

**Reservation prompt** — Pro: moody interior or empty table shot, thin italic overlay ("the table is set"), URL tiny at bottom. Amateur: screenshot of OpenTable. Exemplar: **Frenchette, Casa Cipriani**.

**Hours / operational (happy hour, taco tuesday, brunch)** — Pro: menu-card or ticket aesthetic, all-caps condensed display (KNOCKOUT-family), hairline rules, single accent color, information-only. Amateur: emoji-heavy, "COME SEE US!" Composition: symmetric menu-card on cream. Exemplar: **The NoMad, Carbone matchbook aesthetic**.

**New menu launch** — Pro: carousel — slide 1 cover art (typographic or hero dish), slides 2–6 each dish with name + region + one-sentence description, slide 7 end card. Amateur: one slide cramming every dish. Exemplar: **Noma seasonal drops, King's menu carousels**.

**Behind-the-scenes** — Pro: documentary 35mm film-look photography of hands, fire, prep, sourcing — no graphic overlay or minimal overlay. Amateur: shot-from-phone-in-kitchen with yellow kitchen light. Exemplar: **Blue Hill at Stone Barns, Russ & Daughters archival dumps**.

**Seasonal / holiday** — Pro: single-image statement with restrained type (a mezcal glass for Día de Muertos over a cempasúchil petal scatter + "ofrenda" in script). Amateur: every holiday emoji. Exemplar: **Russ & Daughters Hanukkah series**.

**Carousel** — Pro: treated as a **magazine spread** — slide 1 cover, slide 2 contents/intro, slides 3–n each a single idea, final slide end card. Consistent grid, consistent type. Amateur: slides that look like slideshare. Exemplar: **Order Design's Gage & Tollner carousels, Commission Studio case studies**.

**Event post (dinner, tasting, collab)** — Pro: poster-aesthetic (letterpress, brutalist, or Deco depending on brand), date + chefs + location in typographic stack, 4:5. Amateur: Eventbrite screenshot. Exemplar: **Blue Hill collabs, Misi guest-chef nights**.

**UGC / customer spotlight** — Pro: a single beautifully-shot customer image reframed and typeset with a tiny handle credit; grid preserves brand palette. Amateur: 9 Instagram reposts slapped in a grid. Exemplar: **Rubirosa's family-UGC grid**.

**"The team" / people post** — Pro: environmental portrait, tungsten lighting, apron and tools present, small cap name and role label. Amateur: selfie in kitchen. Exemplar: **Frasca Food & Wine (Studio Mast's work), Gramercy Tavern**.

**Location / ambiance hero** — Pro: wide architectural shot at golden hour, minimal crop, natural light only, no type. Amateur: phone-camera shot with iPhone auto-HDR. Exemplar: **Casa Cipriani, Pastis interior shots**.

**Beverage spotlight (cocktail, mezcal)** — Pro: moody low-light with one rim-light, glass-rim focus, hand entering frame, black or charred background. Amateur: bright cocktail on white. Exemplar: **Handshake Speakeasy, Ghost Donkey, Licorería Limantour**.

**Collage / grid / photo-stack** — Pro: 3- or 4-photo mini-grid with deliberate color harmony, 1px rule between, restrained type. Amateur: Canva "collage" template. Exemplar: **Dimes' overhead flat-lay series**.

**Typographic-only post** — Pro: one word or short phrase as the whole image. Amateur: paragraph of copy. Exemplar: **Atelier Crenn poetic menus, Via Carota "CLOSED MONDAYS" stencil aesthetic**.

**Interactive (this-or-that, polls)** — Pro: two-panel split (50/50) with a single question in display type. Amateur: three stickers crammed on one photo. Exemplar: **King's seasonal A/B, Empellón's taco polls**.

**Storytelling / origin post** — Pro: single archival or family photo + a caption-worthy paragraph; the post feels like a magazine feature. Exemplar: **Russ & Daughters' 4-generation family history posts, Casa Enrique's Chiapas origin stories**.

**Educational (what is mezcal, how to eat a taco)** — Pro: clear diagrammatic post, hand-drawn or clean infographic style, one idea per slide. Exemplar: **Empellón on mole varieties, Sobre Masa on heirloom corn**.

## 7. Voice & personality injection

### Why AI-generic designs feel dead
Specific visual tells of AI/Canva-tier work:
- **Stock-feel linear gradients** (cyan to magenta, sunset pink to purple)
- **Generic sans-serif** (default Poppins, Montserrat, Open Sans — heavily overused)
- **Center-locked symmetrical layouts** with no motion
- **Uncanny food rendering** — over-glossy, plastic-looking, impossible reflections, too-perfect
- **"3D render" over-lit food** with impossible reflections and a single studio-white light
- **Even 33/33/33 color distribution**
- **Identical spacing top, bottom, left, right** — reads like a default template
- **Default Photoshop drop shadows** (90°, 5px, 75% black)
- **Mid-saturation everything** — nothing stands out
- **No texture, no grain, no ink bleed** — too clean is a tell

### Human touches that add life
- **Hand-drawn arrows** (not vector-perfect) — La Bamba's signature
- **Handwritten annotations** scanned and placed with slight rotation
- **Scanned paper textures** — coffee-stained, weathered, creased
- **Taped-photo look** — masking tape at corners, slightly rotated
- **Imperfect rotations** — ±1–3° off-axis rather than locked on grid
- **Offset printing marks** — registration crosses, color bars, grain
- **Ink bleeds and halos** — risograph or letterpress artifacts
- **Torn paper edges** — physical collage feel
- **Circled elements** — red pen over a dish name
- **Stickers stacked over photos** — zine-maximalist

### Personality through saturation and asymmetry
Louder saturation + more asymmetry + more hand-made texture = more personality. Quieter saturation + more symmetry + more restraint = more luxury. Both can be *alive* if committed to. Dead is in the middle.

### Copy voice principles for on-graphic text
- **Short is better.** Headlines 2–6 words.
- **Specific is better than general.** "Shrimp from Isla Mujeres" beats "Fresh seafood."
- **Sensory, not descriptive.** "Smoke, salt, citrus" beats "delicious flavor."
- **Conversational, not corporate.** "Tonight we pour slow" beats "Experience our slow-pour program."
- **One idea per graphic.** Don't cram.

### The "insider's voice"
The best restaurant captions sound like a **friend with taste**, not a brand shouting: "the new carne asada is unreasonable" / "back by demand: the shrimp tostada" / "come before 7 / don't come after 9." It's intimate, confident, slightly underplayed. Avoid: "We are SO excited to announce…" / "Don't miss out!" / "Tag a friend who needs this!" — these are the voice of a brand that hasn't figured out who it is.

### Bilingual content (English / Spanish)
Three reliable layouts:
1. **Stacked hierarchy** — Spanish primary (larger, display serif or script), English secondary (small-caps sans beneath). Feels native / editorial.
2. **Parallel columns** — 50/50 split, Spanish left, English right. Feels bilingual-menu / institutional.
3. **Primary + italic translation** — main phrase in one language, translation in italic below 50% smaller. Most elegant.

Never **intersperse** English and Spanish in the same sentence on-graphic unless the code-switch is the joke ("salud, y'all"). Always match diacritics precisely: **cempasúchil, piña, mañana, salud, niño, panadería**. Wrong diacritics are the #1 bilingual amateur tell.

**Decision cue:** Azteca uses bilingual regularly — Spanish primary, English secondary. Blue Mezcal uses bilingual as accent — one Spanish word/phrase per post as a pull-quote. Jackson House rarely bilingual.

## 8. Research — what top restaurants are doing (2025–26)

### Editorial Italian-American patterns
Dominant grammar: **custom display serif + Söhne/GT America sans**. Spectrum spans from Pujol's near-anti-branding (bone + charcoal + single object) to Cielito Querido Café's maximalist pink-stripe heritage (Cadena + Asoc.). Dominant grammar: **old-style or slab serif + Knockout/Trade Gothic condensed all-caps labels**. ### Fine-dining minimalism
Reductive palette (bone `#F5F2EC`, true white, deep black `#0D0D0D`, one muted accent). Typography tiny — wordmarks under 10% of frame height, wide tracking, lowercase. **70–85% negative space.** Single-image-per-post cadence, slow posting rhythm. Noma, Atelier Crenn, Alinea, EMP, The French Laundry.

### 2025–26 trends relevant to restaurant social
1. **AI-as-tool, not AI-as-tell.** Top studios use AI for moodboarding, upscaling, variant generation, backplate extension — not for hero imagery. Hero food and hero rooms remain *photographed*. If viewers can detect AI, the brand has failed.
2. **Maximalism comeback.** After a decade of minimalism fatigue, ornament returns: custom Deco lettering, gilt, matchbook merch, saturated color, layered texture. &Walsh, MFG, Cadena + Asoc. drive this. Strong fit for Azteca and Blue Mezcal's folk-luxe moments; limited fit for Jackson House beyond seasonal nostalgia.
3. **Editorial magazine carousels.** Instagram carousels designed as magazine spreads — cover, contents, feature, pull-quote, end card. Order Design, Commission Studio, Mast push this. Blue Mezcal's natural home.
4. **90s/00s nostalgia, Y2K chrome.** Chrome type, inflatable letterforms, frosted glass. Mostly in dessert/cocktail bars, not fine dining. A counter-signal for Jackson House (what NOT to do).
5. **Brutalist restaurant branding.** Raw Helvetica, system defaults, receipt aesthetics. Strong in natural wine, sandwich, bakery. Useful as contrast register for a Blue Mezcal wine-bar moment; wrong for Jackson House.
6. **Handcrafted / zine / risograph.** Tape, staples, ink bleeds, handwritten marginalia. Huge 2024–26 in natural wine + coffee + bakery. Applicable to Jackson House specials/events.
7. **Type-as-image.** Oversized display at 50–90% of canvas. Works when names are short/strong. Blue Mezcal can flex this.
8. **Letterbox / anamorphic crops on Reels.** 2.39:1 with heavy black bars. Cinematic register.
9. **Kinetic typography for Reels.** Text-driven Reels synced to audio. Fine dining keeps it restrained; taverns and casual flex more.
10. **Ingredient-forward photography.** The whole raw ingredient photographed with reverence. Blue Hill/Chez Panisse lineage → Sobre Masa, Claro, Quintonil in 2026.
11. **Pantone 2025 COTY Mocha Mousse** `#A47864`: warm brown, perfect for Jackson House. **[Pantone 2026 unverified at time of writing — confirm before anchoring to it.]**

### Testimonial design treatment
- **Star ratings**: set stars in brand accent color, not generic gold. Use five-pointed stars only — never other shapes. Omit the star count entirely if the quote is strong; let the words carry.
- **Quote marks**: oversized hanging left-margin quote ("curly" typographer's quotes only: " and ", never straight "). Size the opening quote at **150–200% of headline cap height**, placed flush-left, baseline aligned with the first word's x-height. Close quote optional.
- **Attribution**: 50% of headline size, italic, small-caps, tracked +40em. Format: *— Critic Name, Publication, 2026*.

### Menu tease formats
- **The "one line" tease**: serif display, single dish name ("OYSTERS & MIGNONETTE"), no photo, one color field. Reads editorial.
- **The matchbook menu**: small card-sized layout, five items max, hairline rules between, all-caps labels + italic descriptors.
- **The chef's note**: handwritten script over an ingredient hero photograph, ~10 words, signed.

# PART III — NANO BANANA / GEMINI IMAGE PROMPT FRAMEWORK

## Model summary
**Current default (Apr 2026):** `gemini-3.1-flash-image-preview` (Nano Banana 2). Use `gemini-3-pro-image-preview` (Nano Banana Pro) when text-heavy or when you need 4K / complex multilingual signage. Use `gemini-2.5-flash-image` (original NB1) only for cheap drafts.

**What it does best:** text rendering in images (industry-leading), multi-turn edits that preserve the rest of the image, character/style consistency across a series, photorealism, and stylized illustration. Aspect ratios 1:1, 4:5, 9:16, 16:9, plus 2K/4K on Pro and NB2. Supports conversational iterative editing — establish a template, then swap content per turn.

**Quirks:** no native negative-prompt field (use semantic negatives in the prompt body). Text under ~25 characters reliable on Flash; Pro handles paragraphs. Specific font *names* not reliably recognized — describe stylistically. Hands and very small text can fail. Silent failures on overloaded prompts — split into multi-turn edits. IMAGE_SAFETY can block alcohol-heavy prompts; rephrase rather than fight. SynthID watermark on every output.

## Prompt framework — the six-variable brief
For reliable professional output, write **one coherent paragraph** covering:

1. **Subject** — who/what, specifically
2. **Action or state** — what is happening
3. **Setting / environment** — surface, room, time of day
4. **Style / medium** — photographic (with camera reference) OR illustration with named movement
5. **Composition / camera** — shot type, lens (35/50/85mm), angle, depth of field, rule-of-thirds in plain English
6. **Lighting** — direction, quality, color temperature (Kelvin), named lighting style

Add to those six, when relevant:
- **Typography overlay** — exact text in straight double quotes, described stylistically
- **Color palette** — hex codes AND named colors
- **Texture / finish** — grain, paper, noise
- **Explicit negative-space callouts** — "clean negative space in upper-third for headline"
- **Aspect ratio** — set via API `imageConfig.aspectRatio`, not in text prompt

### Prompt stem (reusable per brand)
Build a canonical "brand stem" paragraph once, then append scene specifics:

**Azteca stem:**
> "A social media image for Azteca, a family Mexican restaurant. Brand palette: hot pink #FF5BA7, sunshine yellow #FFD400, turquoise #1FB5B0, chile red #E63946, cream #F8F2DF. Typography: bold chunky display sans-serif paired with hand-painted bodega signage and hand-drawn marker arrows. Texture: halftone dots at 45°, subtle risograph misregistration, slight off-axis rotation of stickered elements. **Blue Mezcal stem:**

Brand palette: cobalt blue #1E3A8A, bone cream #F3ECD8, marigold #F2A900, agave green #4E6B3A, charred black #1C1A17. Typography: refined editorial serif headlines with tight tracking, paired with quiet modern grotesque small-caps labels. Texture: fine noise overlay at 5%, subtle handmade paper grain, restrained. ## Prompt engineering playbook (quick reference)

1. **Always use a brand stem** — paste the brand's canonical paragraph first, then scene specifics. This locks palette, type, texture in a reliable way.
2. **Quote exact text in straight double quotes** — every word not quoted is a word the model may paraphrase or misspell.
3. **Describe type stylistically, not by font name** — "refined editorial serif italic" beats "Canela Italic."
4. **State color as both hex AND named descriptor** — "cobalt blue #1E3A8A" reliably locks the swatch.
5. **Call out negative space explicitly** — "clean negative space in the upper third for headline" reliably leaves room.
6. **Set aspect ratio via API parameter**, not prose — `imageConfig.aspectRatio: "4:5"`.
7. **For a series, use multi-turn editing** — establish the template in turn 1, swap only the dish/text in later turns, say "keep everything else identical."
8. **Avoid overloaded prompts** — logo + long menu + three dishes + phone number is a silent-failure recipe. Split into passes.
9. **Name a reference photographer or art movement to anchor mood** — "shot as if by Annie Leibovitz," "editorial like T Magazine," "Portra 800 film emulation."
10. **For legibility of text over food, say "integrated as if printed on the linen"** — not "overlaid" — reduces sticker-look artifacts.
11. **Prefer one brand stem + one scene paragraph at ~100–150 words total** — coherent paragraphs beat keyword lists for Gemini.
12. **Verify bilingual text with a native speaker** — model occasionally misfires on diacritics and idioms.
13. **Never ask for a specific celebrity or recognizable chef's face** — IMAGE_SAFETY blocks.
14. **Restrict alcohol-heavy prompts** to single-glass framing — heavy bar scenes sometimes filter.
15. **Use `gemini-3-pro-image-preview`** for menu posters or anything with paragraphs of text; NB2 for everything else.

## Final principle

Register first. Format second. Copy third. Everything else is grammar. A well-branded Azteca post and a well-branded Blue Mezcal post should feel like they came from different restaurants in different cities, run by different people — because they do. When another Claude reads this file, the first decision is always: **which of the three brands? which register inside it?** Answer that, then the YAML decision rules and the palette, type, and texture follow automatically. The prompt framework is just the export format.

If the output feels AI-generic, check first: is one color doing 60% of the work? Is there negative space or is it mid-density mush? Is there hand-made texture (grain, paper, ink)? Is the type stylistically described with purpose? If any of those is "no," the post is dying at the template step, not the prompt step.

Alive, branded, taste-level work comes from discipline — picking the register, committing to it, and then adding one small human gesture (a hand-drawn arrow, a scanned paper edge, an off-axis rotation, a warm film grain, a Spanish word that pulls the post into specificity). That is how this file works. That is what to generate.
